## Hallucinations and Cognitive Effects
*4 mention(s) · 1 source(s) · single-document*

The theme of "Hallucinations and Cognitive Effects" emerges as a distinctive and recurrent element in the experiences of ultramarathon runners, particularly as documented in the context of the Badwater Ultramarathon. Hallucinations are closely associated with extreme endurance events, and the evidence highlights several interconnected triggers: profound sleep deprivation, severe physical and emotional exhaustion, and the unique nighttime environment of the race. Runners typically experience disrupted sleep, with most hallucinations occurring during the nighttime hours, often around midnight, suggesting that circadian rhythms and darkness further exacerbate cognitive-perceptual disturbances.

Beyond physiological factors, cognitive interpretation plays a substantial role in shaping both the occurrence and intensity of these experiences. The literature and participant interviews underscore that while biological conditions set the stage, a runner’s mindset and perceptual framing can amplify or mitigate the vividness and impact of their hallucinations. Overall, hallucinations in ultramarathons are depicted as complex, multifactorial phenomena arising from the interplay between exhaustion, environmental stressors, and cognitive processes, reflecting the profound psychological demands unique to ultra-endurance running.
